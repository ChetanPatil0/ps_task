Title: Diabetes Mellitus

Diabetes mellitus, commonly known as diabetes, is a group of metabolic disorders characterized by chronic hyperglycemia (high blood sugar) resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. It is a major global health concern and affects millions of people worldwide. There are three main types of diabetes: Type 1, Type 2, and gestational diabetes.

Causes:

* Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disorder where the immune system attacks the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas, leading to little or no insulin production. Genetic factors and environmental triggers may contribute to its development.
* Type 2 diabetes is caused by insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency. Risk factors include obesity, sedentary lifestyle, poor diet, age, and genetic predisposition.
* Gestational diabetes occurs during pregnancy and results from hormonal changes that impair insulin function.

Symptoms:

* Frequent urination (polyuria)
* Excessive thirst (polydipsia)
* Unexplained weight loss
* Fatigue
* Blurred vision
* Slow-healing wounds
* Increased susceptibility to infections

Complications:
If left uncontrolled, diabetes can lead to long-term complications affecting various organs:

* Cardiovascular disease (heart attack, stroke)
* Neuropathy (nerve damage)
* Nephropathy (kidney damage)
* Retinopathy (eye damage leading to blindness)
* Foot ulcers and infections

Diagnosis:

* Fasting plasma glucose test
* Oral glucose tolerance test
* Glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) test

Treatment:

* Lifestyle modifications (healthy diet, regular exercise)
* Medications (oral hypoglycemic drugs)
* Insulin therapy for Type 1 or advanced Type 2 diabetes
* Regular monitoring of blood glucose levels

Prevention and Management:

* Maintain a balanced diet rich in fiber, vegetables, and whole grains
* Engage in regular physical activity
* Avoid excessive sugar and processed foods
* Monitor blood sugar and blood pressure regularly
* Educate oneself about disease management
